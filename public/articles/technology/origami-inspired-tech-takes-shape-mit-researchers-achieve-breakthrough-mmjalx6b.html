Origami-Inspired Tech Takes Shape: MIT Researchers Achieve Breakthrough

The Hidden Reporter Staff March 3, 2026 Source: BBC Technology

Screams of joy filled the laboratory as Akib Zaman, a PhD candidate at Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), successfully created a mini chair from a flat, rectangular piece of material seemingly out of nowhere.

The achievement marked the first time Zaman and his fellow researchers had seen their idea come to life after months of work.

Zaman was inspired by the Japanese art form kirigami, which involves cutting and folding paper to achieve 3D shapes, unlike origami, which only involves folding.

The team developed a way of 3D-printing material divided into chunky, square-shaped tiles, with precise cuts that allow them to pop up into a desired 3D shape when squeezed together.

They created a computer program that converts a 3D model into a flat, grid-like version, to which a pull-cord is attached.

The technology has potential applications in making larger structures, such as buildings, or tiny structures that can open up and deliver drugs to specific sites in the body.

However, experts note that scaling up origami-based designs and using them with materials other than paper can be challenging.

Despite this, researchers believe that origami-inspired tech is on the cusp of being used in real engineering applications.
