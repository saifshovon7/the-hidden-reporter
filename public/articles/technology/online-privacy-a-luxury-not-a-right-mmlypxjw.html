Online Privacy: A Luxury, Not a Right?

Thomas Bunting and Prof Alan Woodward discuss the state of online privacy, with Bunting saying it's a luxury, not a right, and Woodward arguing that people should care about it to protect their freedom and democracy.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 5, 2026 · Source: BBC Technology

In 2026, online privacy is a luxury, not a right, according to Thomas Bunting, an analyst at the UK innovation think tank Nesta.

Bunting was discussing advertising and a potential future where smart fridges in kitchens could share information about their owners' dietary choices with health insurers.

While this may seem like a dystopian reality, Bunting believes it's not inevitable and that people need to understand the implications of their online activities.

He recalls a conversation with his teacher at around 15, where no one in the class believed privacy was an important principle to protect.

Experiences like this trouble veteran advocates for online privacy, such as cybersecurity expert Prof Alan Woodward from Surrey University.

Woodward argues that people should care about online privacy because it shapes who has power over their lives and that caring about privacy is not about having something to hide, but about having something to protect.

According to Woodward, this includes freedom of thought, experimentation, dissent, and personal development without permanent surveillance.

He also notes that when people assume they are constantly tracked, they self-censor, which can harm free speech and weaken democracy.

Woodward's views are not unique, as Scott McNealy, co-founder of Sun Microsystems, famously said in 1999, 'You have zero privacy anyway. Get over it.'

Despite the tech sector's history of breaking through privacy boundaries, it has also created tools meant to protect us, such as private web browsers, encrypted messaging apps, password managers, tracker blockers, and Virtual Private Networks.

However, according to data analysis firm Statista, in 2024, more than 1.35 billion people were affected by data compromised through a breach, hack, or exposure.
