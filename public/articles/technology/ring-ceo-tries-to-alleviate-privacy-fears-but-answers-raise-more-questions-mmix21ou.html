Ring CEO Tries to Alleviate Privacy Fears, but Answers Raise More Questions

The Hidden Reporter Staff March 9, 2026 Source: TechCrunch

Ring founder and CEO Jamie Siminoff has been attempting to calm concerns about privacy since the Super Bowl, but his explanations may not have provided the reassurance he intended.

Siminoff spoke with TechCrunch days after the company's first-ever Super Bowl commercial aired, introducing Search Party – an AI-powered feature that uses Ring camera footage to help find lost dogs.

However, the TV spot sparked a firestorm, and Siminoff has been making the rounds on various media outlets, including CNN and the New York Times, to explain that his critics misunderstand Ring's intentions.

The feature at the center of the controversy involves a dog going missing and Ring alerting nearby camera owners to ask whether the animal appears in their footage. Users can respond or ignore the request entirely and remain invisible to everyone involved.

Siminoff emphasized that doing nothing is equivalent to opting out and that no one is forced into participating. He compared it to finding a dog in your backyard and deciding whether to contact the owner.

Despite his efforts to alleviate concerns, Siminoff acknowledged that a visual in the Super Bowl spot – a map showing blue circles pulsing outward from house to house as cameras switched on across a neighborhood grid – may have contributed to the backlash.

Ring chose a sensitive moment to make its case, as the disappearance of 84-year-old Nancy Guthrie from her Tucson home in late January had raised concerns about home surveillance cameras and safety.

Siminoff leaned into the Guthrie case, suggesting that more cameras on more houses could have helped solve the case. He noted that Ring's network had turned up footage of a suspicious vehicle two and a half miles from the Guthrie property.
