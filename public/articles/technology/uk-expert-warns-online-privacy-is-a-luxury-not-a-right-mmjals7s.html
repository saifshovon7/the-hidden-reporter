UK Expert Warns Online Privacy is a Luxury, Not a Right

The Hidden Reporter Staff March 5, 2026 Source: BBC Technology

Thomas Bunting, an analyst at the UK innovation think tank Nesta, believes that online privacy is a luxury, not a right. He suggests that people have been conditioned to accept the use of their data as currency in exchange for services like social media.

Bunting, who is 25, recalls a conversation with his teacher at the age of 15, where not a single student believed that privacy was an important principle to protect. This experience has left a lasting impression on him, and he worries that people are not taking online privacy seriously enough.

Prof Alan Woodward, a cybersecurity expert from Surrey University, shares Bunting's concerns. He argues that people should care about online privacy because it shapes who has power over their lives. According to Woodward, caring about privacy is not about having something to hide, but about having something to protect, such as freedom of thought, experimentation, and dissent.

Woodward's views are supported by the fact that many people are self-censoring due to the fear of being tracked and shamed. For example, a young influencer once shared with him that her friends avoid dancing in clubs because they are afraid of being filmed and shamed.

The tech sector has a long history of breaking through privacy boundaries, but it has also created tools to protect us. However, despite the existence of private web browsers, encrypted messaging apps, password managers, tracker blockers, and Virtual Private Networks, data breaches and hacks continue to affect millions of people.

In 2024, over 1.35 billion people were affected by data compromised through a breach, hack, or exposure, according to Statista. This raises questions about the effectiveness of online privacy tools and the need for further regulation.
