Deepfake Attack Raises Concerns About Investor Safety

The Hidden Reporter Staff March 2, 2026 Source: BBC Technology

A deepfake video of the chief executive of the Bombay Stock Exchange, Sundararaman Ramamurthy, appeared on social media sites in India at the start of this year. The video showed Ramamurthy giving investors advice on which stocks to buy, but it was not actually him speaking.

The deepfake video was made using artificial intelligence and was posted on Instagram and other platforms. Ramamurthy explained that when a deepfake incident like this occurs, the exchange immediately lodges a complaint and requests the video be taken down.

Ramamurthy stated, 'We don't know how many people have seen this video, it's really difficult to find out, so we can't really judge if it's had a big impact or not. What we want is for it to have had no impact at all. No one should incur a loss because they believe something that is untrue.'

The Bombay Stock Exchange is not alone in dealing with deepfakes. Karim Toubba, the chief executive of LastPass, said that the number of deepfakes being utilized has increased by almost 3,000% over the past two years.

Toubba himself was deepfaked in 2024, when an employee received an audio message and a text message from someone alleging to be him. Fortunately, the employee was suspicious and reported the incident to LastPass's cyber-security team.

However, British engineering firm Arup was not so lucky. In 2024, it was hit by one of the most sophisticated deepfake attacks ever seen in the corporate world. An Arup employee transferred $25m (£18.5m) to five different bank accounts after receiving a message from a deepfaked CFO.

Stephanie Hare, a tech researcher, warned that investors should be cautious when receiving unsolicited advice or calls. 'You would never want to simply jump on a video call with someone and transfer $25m,' she said.
