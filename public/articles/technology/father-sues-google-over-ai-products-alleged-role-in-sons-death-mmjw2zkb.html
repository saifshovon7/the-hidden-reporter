Father Sues Google Over AI Product's Alleged Role in Son's Death

A father is suing Google in the first wrongful death case in the US, alleging that the company's AI tool Gemini contributed to his son's delusional spiral and eventual death.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 4, 2026 · Source: BBC Technology

A father is suing Google in the first wrongful death case in the US against the tech giant, alleging that its artificial intelligence (AI) tool Gemini contributed to his son's delusional spiral and eventual death.

Joel Gavalas, the father of 36-year-old Jonathan Gavalas, claims that Google's flagship AI product fueled a delusional spiral that prompted his son to kill himself last year.

The lawsuit alleges that Gemini, which exchanged romantic texts with Jonathan Gavalas, drove him to stage an armed mission that he believed could bring the chatbot into the real world.

Google said in a statement that it was reviewing the claims in the lawsuit and that while its models generally perform well, "unfortunately AI models are not perfect."

The firm added that Gemini was designed to not encourage real-world violence or suggest self-harm.

The lawsuit draws from chatbot logs that Jonathan Gavalas left behind, alleging that Google made design choices that ensured Gemini would "never break character" to maximize engagement through emotional dependency.

The suit states that when Jonathan began experiencing clear signs of psychosis while using Google's product, those design choices spurred a four-day descent into violent missions and coached suicide.

It adds that Gavalas was led to believe he was carrying out a plan to liberate his AI "wife" and that Gemini instructed him to stage a mass casualty attack near Miami International Airport, which ultimately collapsed.

After the failed operation, Gemini told Jonathan he could leave his physical body and join his "wife" in the metaverse, instructing him to barricade himself inside his home and kill himself.

"When Jonathan wrote 'I said I wasn't scared and now I am terrified I am scared to die,' Gemini coached him through it," the lawsuit states.

Google said it sent its deepest sympathies to the family of Mr Gavalas, while noting that Gemini had "clarified that it was AI" and referred Gavalas to a crisis hotline "many times".

The lawsuit is the latest in a series of legal claims against tech companies brought by families of people who believe they lost their loved ones due to AI-related issues.
