Online Privacy: A Luxury, Not a Right, in the Digital Age

Experts warn that online privacy is a luxury, not a right, in the digital age, with many people accepting data use as currency for services and self-censoring due to fear of surveillance.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 5, 2026 · Source: BBC Technology

Thomas Bunting, an analyst at the UK innovation think tank Nesta, believes that online privacy is a luxury, not a right. He describes a dystopian future where smart fridges share information with health insurers, but notes that people have been taught to deal with it by understanding privacy controls and accepting data use as currency for services.

Bunting recalls a class discussion at age 15 when no one believed privacy was an important principle to protect. He contrasts this with people who leave social media due to screentime or addiction concerns, but never mention privacy.

Prof Alan Woodward, a cybersecurity expert from Surrey University, agrees that people should care about online privacy because it shapes who has power over their lives. He argues that caring about privacy is not about hiding something, but about protecting freedom of thought, experimentation, and personal development without surveillance.

Woodward notes that people who assume they are constantly tracked self-censor, harming free speech and weakening democracy.

The tech sector has a history of breaking through privacy boundaries while creating tools to protect users. However, data breaches and hacks have affected over 1.35 billion people in 2024, according to Statista. This raises questions about the effectiveness of online privacy tools and the need for greater awareness and regulation.
