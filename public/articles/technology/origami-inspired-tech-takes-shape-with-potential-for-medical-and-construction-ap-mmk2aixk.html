Origami-Inspired Tech Takes Shape, With Potential for Medical and Construction Applications

Researchers at MIT have developed a technique inspired by origami and kirigami, which can create 3D shapes from flat materials. The technology has potential applications in construction and medicine, but faces challenges in scaling up and using materials other than paper.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 3, 2026 · Source: BBC Technology

A PhD candidate at Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) has successfully created a mini chair using a technique inspired by origami and kirigami, a Japanese art form that involves cutting and folding paper.

Akib Zaman and his colleagues developed a 3D-printing method that divides material into chunky, square-shaped tiles, which can be compressed together to form a desired 3D shape.

The team created a computer program that converts a 3D model into a flat, grid-like version, allowing them to achieve the desired shape with a simple pull-cord.

According to Zaman, the technology has potential applications in construction, where it could be used to create larger structures like buildings, as well as in medicine, where it could be used to deliver drugs to specific sites in the body.

However, experts note that origami-inspired engineering still faces challenges, including scaling up designs and using materials other than paper, which is extremely forgiving and can be folded and re-folded many times without losing its shape.

Zaman and his colleagues are now collaborating on research in the area of medical applications, while experts like Mark Schenk from the University of Bristol emphasize the need for further development and experimentation to make origami-inspired tech more practical and widely applicable.
