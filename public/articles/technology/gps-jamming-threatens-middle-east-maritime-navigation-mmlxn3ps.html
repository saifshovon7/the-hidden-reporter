GPS Jamming Threatens Middle East Maritime Navigation

GPS jamming in the Middle East is causing concern for maritime navigation, with hundreds of ships mysteriously clustered in the wrong locations in waters off Iran, the UAE, and Qatar.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 10, 2026 · Source: BBC Technology

Hundreds of ships are mysteriously clustered in the wrong locations in waters off Iran, the United Arab Emirates, and Qatar, sparking concerns about GPS jamming in the region.

Michelle Wiese Bockmann, a senior maritime intelligence analyst at Windward, a maritime AI company, has observed 35 different clusters of ships, which she believes are the result of disrupted GPS coordinates.

The clusters, which appear as tight, unnatural circles of icons on a map, are a clear indication that something is amiss, as ships do not typically bunch together in such a manner.

GPS jamming can cause significant disruption to maritime navigation, hampered communications, and potentially deadly accidents.

The issue is not new, as GPS jamming has affected aircraft in Europe and has been a feature of the war in Ukraine.

However, the current conflict in the Middle East has brought electronic warfare to a broader area, with the National Hydrographic Office Pakistan warning about interference affecting shipping in the region.

Ships use Automatic Identification Systems (AIS) to avoid one another, but GPS jamming can disrupt this system, increasing the risk of collisions, especially at night or in poor visibility.

Alan Woodward, a cybersecurity expert at the University of Surrey, notes that the problem is not just about knowing where one is going, but also about knowing where others are going.

While there is no official confirmation of who is behind the jamming, military analysts suspect Iran, which has threatened to attack any ship attempting to pass through the Strait of Hormuz.

Thomas Withington, an associate fellow at the Royal United Services Institute, believes that Iran's GPS jamming tools are likely to be domestically produced or made with equipment sourced from Russia or China.
