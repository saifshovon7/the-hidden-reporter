States Crack Down on Surveillance Pricing Practices

The Hidden Reporter Staff March 7, 2026 Source: Gizmodo.com

Democrats in Pennsylvania have introduced a bill to stop retailers from rapidly changing the price of essential goods and services within a 24-hour period, a practice known as surveillance pricing.

The bill, Senate Bill 1205, aims to amend the state's Unfair Trade Practices and Consumer Protection Law to include any rapid changes to prices on essential goods within a 24-hour period.

At least a dozen states are considering legislation this year around the topic of surveillance pricing, which involves using high-tech tools to change prices for different consumers.

Surveillance pricing has long been a thing online, where retailers can easily show different prices to different consumers, but consumers at brick-and-mortar stores have become increasingly concerned about how digital price tags and cameras in stores could be used to change prices.

Retailers can hypothetically use the same tools to charge different prices based on age, race, gender, and past buying history, among a host of other variables.

George Slover, Senior Counsel for Competition Policy at the Center for Democracy and Technology, testified this week about the proposed legislation in Pennsylvania.

Slover studies antitrust issues and has a keen interest in algorithmic collusion and what he calls bespoke pricing.

New York became the first state to pass a law around dynamic pricing with the Algorithmic Pricing Disclosure Act, which requires disclosure when an algorithm is being used with your personal data to set a price.

Companies like Instacart have been caught experimenting with AI to see how much consumers would pay for various groceries, with some people paying up to 23% more for the same goods.

Ridesharing companies like Uber can also charge users more when they have lower battery life on their phone.
