London School Wars: How Social Media Misinformation Exposed Parent-Child Gap

A series of social media posts calling for 'red v blue' wars between London schools sparked a police presence, but investigation reveals a modest reaction from young people and a significant response from parents.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 10, 2026 · Source: BBC News

On a typical Friday, February 27th, secondary schools in Southwark and Croydon in south London should have been quiet after lessons ended. However, teachers took positions on streets around the schools, with some cancelling after-school detentions to allow pupils to go home early. Police officers were also present, equipped with dispersal orders to manage potential gatherings. The reason behind this unusual scene was a series of social media posts calling for 'red v blue' wars between schools across the city.

The posts, which began circulating on TikTok and Snapchat, featured images of people in balaclavas, weapons, and lists of school names. Despite the potential for violence, no actual incidents were reported, according to the Metropolitan Police. The incident appears to be another example of social media's influence on young people, but further investigation reveals a different story.

Tracking down the original posts and speaking to individuals within social media companies showed that young people initially reacted modestly to the posts. However, the real interest began when the posts were shared across parent WhatsApp groups. Comments on the viral posters revealed teenagers joking about their allegiance, stating they had only heard about the posts from their parents' group chats.

Snapchat removed a small number of posts for violating community guidelines, while TikTok reported only a small number of searches for School Wars until it received wider attention from parents and media. A police detective, speaking on condition of anonymity, noted, 'We have to take it seriously when parents and kids do – even if the people who came up with it weren't that serious.'

The incident raises questions about the concept of phantom trends, where fear can exacerbate real-world impact rather than mitigating it. It also highlights the gap between how children and their parents experience social media.
