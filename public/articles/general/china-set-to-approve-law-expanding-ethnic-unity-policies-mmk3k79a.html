China Set to Approve Law Expanding 'Ethnic Unity' Policies

China is set to approve a new law expanding 'ethnic unity' policies, which critics say will further restrict the rights of minority groups and their way of life.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 10, 2026 · Source: BBC News

For decades, the Chinese government has been accused of repressive policies aimed at subjugating ethnic minorities, forcing them to assimilate into the dominant Han culture.

A new law set to be approved through China's annual parliamentary session will solidify, expand and speed up this process, threatening the rights of minority groups and their way of life, academics and human rights activists say.

The Chinese government defends the law as crucial for promoting 'modernisation through greater unity' and calls it the law for 'Promoting Ethnic Unity and Progress', but critics argue it will further restrict the rights of minority groups.

The law lowers the status of other languages at the expense of Mandarin, encourages intermarriage between the dominant Han Chinese and other ethnicities, and requires parents to 'educate and guide minors to love the Chinese Communist Party', among other provisions.

Experts see the law as an entrenchment of what has already become a core part of Chinese President Xi Jinping's rule, including the 'Sinicisation of religion', which requires religious practices to conform with what the Communist Party deems to be Chinese culture and values.

With 55 official ethnic minorities in China, Beijing has always worried more about some than others, facing grave allegations of human rights violations in Xinjiang and Tibet.

The Communist Party has relied on promoting fear of retribution to combat any example of dangerous talk of breaking away, rather than offering more autonomy to make minority groups happier to be part of China.

A history of crackdowns on ethnic minorities in China includes the 2008 Tibetan uprising, deadly clashes in Xinjiang in 2009, and a 2013 car bomb attack in Tiananmen Square.
