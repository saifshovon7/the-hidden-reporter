Global Oil Prices Soar Amid Iran Conflict

The US and Israel's war in Iran is causing a global oil supply shock, sending prices soaring toward $85 per barrel and driving up prices at the pump.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 9, 2026 · Source: BBC News

The impact of the US and Israel's war in Iran is starting to hit home, affecting energy markets worldwide. As the conflict blocks oil exports from the Gulf region and producers cut output, the supply shock has sent oil prices soaring toward $85 per barrel, rattling financial markets and driving up prices at the pump.

The war has highlighted the world's ongoing dependence on the Middle East for energy supplies, recalling supply shocks from the 1950s and 1970s. Analysts say the current impact is much larger, with roughly 20% of the world's crude supply traveling through the Strait of Hormuz, where ship traffic has been blocked.

Producers outside the region, such as the US, Brazil, and Norway, have limited ability to ramp up production, forcing them to announce cuts. In Iraq, output is down by more than 60%, while Kuwait and the United Arab Emirates are also scaling back.

The energy strains are not limited to oil, as about 20% of the world's natural gas supplies are also down, following Qatar's state energy firm halting production due to military attacks. Analysts at JP Morgan expect "visible shortages" to emerge in Asia and Europe within a week.

Some governments have already announced price caps and rationing measures, with universities in Bangladesh closing early for the Eid al-Fitr holidays. In the UK, Chancellor Rachel Reeves has warned of the risks of an inflationary shock.

While some countries have discussed plans to release oil reserves to relieve the crisis, Hunter Kornfeind, senior macro energy analyst of Rapid Energy Group, said the scale of any release would be "peanuts" in comparison to the demand.

The supply shock has already led to higher energy prices, with Brent crude and the US benchmark, West Texas Intermediate, surging since the war began. Natural gas prices in the UK and Europe have almost doubled since before the war, and prices at the pump in the US are approaching $3.50 per gallon, up from roughly $2.90 a month ago.
