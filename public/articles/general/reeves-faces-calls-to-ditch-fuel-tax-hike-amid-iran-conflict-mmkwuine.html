Reeves Faces Calls to Ditch Fuel Tax Hike Amid Iran Conflict

UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves faces growing calls from opposition parties to reverse a planned hike to fuel duty amid the ongoing conflict in Iran.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 10, 2026 · Source: BBC News

UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves is facing growing pressure from opposition parties to reverse a planned hike to fuel duty as the conflict in Iran continues.

The tax rate on petrol and diesel is set to rise from September, when a 5p cut made after Russia's invasion of Ukraine is phased out. However, Reform UK, the Conservatives, and the Liberal Democrats are arguing that the war in Iran means the chancellor should keep the cut in place.

Energy prices have risen since the US and Israel began strikes on the country, amid fears the conflict would cause lengthy disruption to supplies. The situation has led to calls for a rethink on the fuel duty hike, with Reform UK proposing a series of cuts to green levies to cover the cost to the Treasury.

Reform UK's Treasury spokesman, Robert Jenrick, said the situation in the Middle East made it the "worst possible moment" for fuel duty to rise. He was speaking alongside Reform leader Nigel Farage, who branded the government's green levies "lunatic" and hit out at Labour's policies.

The Conservatives have also published a motion to trigger a future vote on the issue. Chancellor Reeves has said that the "most important thing" ministers can do to keep fuel prices down is to help "de-escalate" the conflict.

She added that the government has launched a scheme to provide motorists with real-time data on forecourt prices and hinted that she could raise the tax-free amount drivers can claim back in mileage expenses in the future.

The 5p cut to fuel duty was announced by Tory Chancellor Rishi Sunak in 2022 after Russia's invasion of Ukraine caused a worldwide spike in energy costs. It was extended in 2023 by the previous Conservative government and again by Labour upon entering office.

Reeves prolonged the cut again at last year's Budget, alongside plans to phase it out from September. The 5p reduction will be fully unwound by March 2027.
