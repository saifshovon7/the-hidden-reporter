SEND Reforms Criticized for Weakening Children's Legal Protections

The government's plans to reform the SEND system in England have been criticized by charity IPSEA, who warn that the reforms will 'strip away' legal protections for children and young people needing support.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 9, 2026 · Source: BBC News

The Independent Provider of Special Education Advice (IPSEA), a charity specialising in English SEND law, has warned that the government's plans to reform the special educational needs and disabilities (SEND) system in England will 'strip away' legal protections for children and young people needing support.

The charity, which welcomed some reforms, including plans to boost funding and specialist support in mainstream schools, expressed concerns that improvements could be made without weakening the legal rights families rely on.

Under current rules, if a child or young person needs more support than is typically provided in mainstream schools, their parent or carer can apply for an education, health and care plan (EHCP). This identifies their needs and sets out the support they should receive.

However, under government plans, only children with the most complex needs will qualify for EHCPs by 2035, and the support they will be legally entitled to will be set out in new specialist provision packages.

IPSEA is concerned that this means the support a child or young person receives will depend on which 'band' of package they qualify for, rather than their individual needs.

The charity is also concerned that the legal rights attached to new Individual Support Plans (ISPs) aren't as strong as the rights that come with an EHCP.

Under the reforms, all children and young people with SEND will have the legal right to an ISP, but IPSEA warns that there won't be a clear, enforceable duty to deliver the provision laid out in an ISP.

According to IPSEA, a parent who is unhappy with their child's ISP would have to go through a new school complaints system, before being able to complain to the Education Secretary.

The charity's chief executive, Madeleine Cassidy, said that legal rights are not optional extras and any new system must ensure that support is statutory, enforceable and backed by a clear right of appeal.

IPSEA is concerned that the reforms will weaken individual rights to support and that improvements could be made without stripping away legal protections.
