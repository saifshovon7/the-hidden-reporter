Cocoa Farmers in Ghana and Ivory Coast Struggle as Chocolate Market Slumps

Cocoa farmers in Ghana and Ivory Coast are struggling due to the slump in the chocolate market, with many not being paid for months and facing financial difficulties.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 9, 2026 · Source: BBC News

Cocoa farmers in Ghana and Ivory Coast are facing a dire situation due to the slump in the chocolate market. The price of chocolate bars has increased globally over the past year, but the farmers have not seen a corresponding increase in their earnings.

In fact, many farmers have not been paid for months, leading to desperation. Akosua Frimpong, a 52-year-old Ghanaian cocoa farmer, shared a heart-wrenching story with the BBC. Her husband fell ill, and she couldn't afford to take him to the hospital, resulting in his death at home.

The recent collapse in prices has made cocoa beans 40% more expensive than what international traders are willing to pay. This is partly due to a good harvest worldwide, which coincided with lower demand. Additionally, chocolate makers have been using less cocoa due to the high prices in the past, leading to smaller chocolate bars.

The knock-on effect should eventually lead to cheaper chocolate bars, but it has left the cocoa industry in Ghana and Ivory Coast in shambles. Even when prices were high, West Africa's cocoa farmers felt they were not benefiting from the world's chocolate lovers.

Their farms are located in remote areas with poor infrastructure, and they live in villages with limited access to basic amenities like electricity and running water. The situation is dire, and farmers like Frimpong are struggling to make ends meet.

Cocobod, the Ghana Cocoa Board, has stepped in to buy much of the cocoa to prevent the industry from collapsing. However, many farmers claim they haven't been paid. The payment delay is affecting around 800,000 cocoa farmers and has a ripple effect on hundreds of thousands of rural livelihoods.

Last October, Cocobod set the amount to be paid to farmers at nearly $5,300 (£3,900) per tonne, but the global market price has fallen below this level. The shortfall has added to the board's debt, which now amounts to some $3bn.

In response to the financial difficulties, Cocobod officials took a pay cut in February – 20% for the executive management and 10% for senior staff.
