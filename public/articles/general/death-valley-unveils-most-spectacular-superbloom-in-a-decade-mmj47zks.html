Death Valley Unveils Most Spectacular Superbloom in a Decade

The Hidden Reporter Staff March 8, 2026 Source: NBC News

A spectacular superbloom of wildflowers has transformed the normally barren landscape of Death Valley National Park into a vibrant display of colors. The bloom, which is the most extensive in a decade, is a result of rainier-than-normal conditions throughout the region last fall and early winter.

According to the National Park Service, Death Valley typically receives only about 2 inches of rainfall each year. However, from November through early January, the park received about 2.5 inches of rain, exceeding its annual average in just two and a half months.

Abby Wines, acting deputy superintendent at Death Valley National Park, explained that superblooms only occur after especially wet fall and winter seasons. The most extensive blooms need the 'right' type of rain, which includes multiple days of drizzly, foggy, gentle rain that soaks in, but not heavy monsoon rains.

David Blacker, executive director of the nonprofit Death Valley Natural History Association, described the area as 'an amazing landscape of colors' and praised the 'amazing smell' of the wildflowers. He noted that visitors can see blooms all along Badwater Road, particularly the southern end, and recommended reaching less populated viewpoints by hiking.

Erik Rakestraw, a curator of botany at the Arizona-Sonora Desert Museum in Tucson, explained that the types of wildflowers that bloom in the desert are known as ephemerals. These flowers can exist for long periods of time in seed form in the soil and can germinate in the proper conditions.

For anyone hoping to catch this year's superbloom, time is of the essence. Wines said the wildflowers at lower elevations are only expected to persist until mid- to late March, while blooms at higher elevations are forecast to emerge from around April to June, depending on weather conditions.
