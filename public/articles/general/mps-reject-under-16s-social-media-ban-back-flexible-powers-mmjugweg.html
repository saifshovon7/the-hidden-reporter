MPs Reject Under-16s Social Media Ban, Back Flexible Powers

MPs have rejected a proposal to ban social media for under-16s, opting for more flexible ministerial powers instead. The decision has sparked criticism from some who argue that the government has failed to take adequate action to protect children's safety online.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 9, 2026 · Source: BBC News

MPs have rejected an Australia-style ban on social media for under-16s, opting instead for more flexible ministerial powers.

The proposed ban, which would have prohibited sites like TikTok, Instagram, and Snapchat for children, was brought forward as part of the Children's Wellbeing and Schools Bill.

However, education minister Olivia Bailey urged MPs to dismiss the change and support more flexible restrictions, which would give Science Secretary Liz Kendall the power to restrict or ban children of certain ages from accessing social media services and chat bots.

The alternative plan will also allow Kendall to limit access to specific features that are harmful or addictive on social media, as well as restrict or limit children's Virtual Private Network (VPN) use and change the age of digital consent in the UK.

MPs voted 307 to 173 against the Lords proposal for an outright ban, supporting Bailey's bid instead.

While more than 100 Labour MPs abstained from the vote, the Liberal Democrats criticized the decision, saying failure to commit to a ban was 'simply not good enough'.

The debate on social media regulation and children's safety continues, with the government launching a consultation to seek views on the issue.

Some supporters of the ban, including actor Hugh Grant, argue that it would help protect young people from the negative effects of social media, while critics, such as the National Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children (NSPCC), warn of the potential consequences of driving children towards less regulated corners of the internet.

The father of Molly Russell, who took her own life at 14 after viewing harmful content online, also spoke out against the ban, saying the government should focus on robust enforcement of existing laws.
