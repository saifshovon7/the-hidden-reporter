How the Iran War May Impact Your Finances

The US-Israeli war with Iran is having a significant impact on people's finances in the UK, with rising petrol prices, mortgage rate increases, and potential effects on energy bills and heating oil costs.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 9, 2026 · Source: BBC News

The ongoing US-Israeli war with Iran has already had a significant impact on people's finances in the UK, with various areas of concern emerging. From petrol prices to mortgage rates, the duration of the conflict and the speed of supply line and economic recovery will determine the depth and sustainability of these effects.

One of the immediate consequences has been a rise in petrol prices, with the average cost increasing by 4.68p to 137.51p per litre and diesel by 8.59p to 150.97p, according to the RAC motoring organisation. Analysts predict that every $10 increase in oil prices will push up pump prices by approximately 7p per litre, making average petrol prices of over 140p per litre inevitable, and potentially breaching 150p per litre if oil prices do not fall.

While motoring organisations have encouraged drivers to reduce non-essential journeys and adopt fuel-efficient driving styles, the impact of rising petrol prices is not limited to motorists. Higher transport costs for supermarkets could lead to increased prices for goods and services, including food.

Before the war, there was a hope for a steady decline in interest rates for new, fixed mortgages, as well as lower variable rates. However, the opposite is now happening, with some of the UK's largest lenders raising rates due to rising funding costs and an expectation that the base borrowing rate will not fall as anticipated.

Mortgage rate increases have been observed for homeowners obtaining new two or five-year deals or renewing existing ones, but not to a significant extent. Nevertheless, there are concerns about "painful" rises to come, particularly for borrowers seeking shorter-term deals. The average rate on a two-year deal has risen to 4.87%, and the average five-year fix is up to 4.98%, according to Moneyfacts.

At times of economic uncertainty, lenders may withdraw mortgage products from the market, reducing consumer choice. A couple of lenders have already removed their entire ranges, anticipating repricing at a higher level.

Adam French, head of consumer finance at Moneyfacts, noted that when lenders pull deals instead of simply adjusting pricing, it often indicates that funding costs have moved too quickly for incremental changes to keep pace.

Regarding energy bills and heating oil costs, there is some protection in place for household gas and electricity bills, thanks to the price cap set by energy regulator Ofgem. However, this protection is time-limited and does not cover everyone.

The maximum price for each unit of gas and electricity is capped, but households that use more energy than the average may not benefit from this protection. Additionally, the cap does not cover heating oil costs, which are expected to rise in response to the conflict.
