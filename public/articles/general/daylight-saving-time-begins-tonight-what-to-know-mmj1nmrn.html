Daylight Saving Time Begins Tonight: What to Know

The Hidden Reporter Staff March 7, 2026 Source: Yahoo Entertainment

Daylight saving time starts tonight at 2 a.m. local time, with clocks across most of the United States springing forward one hour. This change will result in an hour of lost sleep and push sunset and sunrise an hour later until the clocks fall back in November.

While many people celebrate the extra daylight at the end of the day, daylight saving time is not without controversy. Some lawmakers want to end the practice of changing clocks entirely.

So, why do we have daylight saving time, and what could happen with it down the line? Here's what to know.

In the United States, daylight saving time begins at 2 a.m. local time on Sunday, March 8, when clocks move forward one hour. It ends on Sunday, November 1, when clocks move back one hour.

Not all U.S. states observe daylight saving time. Hawaii and most of Arizona, with the exception of the Navajo Nation, do not participate. Additionally, U.S. territories such as American Samoa, Guam, the Northern Mariana Islands, Puerto Rico, and the Virgin Islands also do not observe daylight saving time.

While daylight saving time is observed worldwide, not every country participates. In North America, much of Canada and parts of Mexico near the U.S. border participate. British Columbia's Premier David Eby announced that the province is switching to permanent daylight time, adopting the "Pacific Time" zone.

Most of Europe practices daylight saving time, with the exception of countries like Iceland, Russia, and Belarus. In Africa and Asia, only a few countries observe daylight saving time, including Israel, Lebanon, Cyprus, and Egypt.

Even in countries that observe daylight saving time, start and end dates vary, so it's essential to check how locals operate.

Daylight saving time was created in 1918 to save on fuel costs during World War I. It was reinstated during World War II and the early 1970s energy crisis. The Uniform Time Act of 1966 made daylight saving time federal law, creating a standard national schedule for participating states.

Many people are unhappy with daylight saving time due to the interruption it causes to sleep, which can have serious health consequences. Research suggests that the practice can lead to increased strokes, heart attacks, and other health issues.
