Gerry Adams Faces Civil Court Case Over IRA Bombings

The Hidden Reporter Staff March 9, 2026 Source: BBC News

A civil court case brought by three IRA bomb victims against former Sinn Féin leader Gerry Adams has opened in London. The men are seeking a ruling that he is personally liable for decisions to plant car bombs in London and Manchester in 1973 and 1996.

Adams has stated he had 'no direct or indirect' involvement and has repeatedly denied ever being in the IRA. However, the claimants are suing him for a nominal £1 in damages for 'vindicatory purposes.'

The civil case is being heard by Mr Justice Swift at the Royal Courts of Justice and is listed to last for seven days. The action has been brought by John Clark, a victim of the Old Bailey attack in 1973, as well as Jonathan Ganesh and Barry Laycock, who were injured in the 1996 attacks in London's Docklands and at Manchester's Arndale Shopping Centre.

The three bombings were the first and last the IRA carried out in Britain. More than 200 people were injured in the bomb left outside the Old Bailey in 1973, while two people were killed and many others injured by an IRA bomb in London's Docklands in 1996. Just months later, a bomb exploded near Manchester Arndale shopping centre, injuring over 200 people.

The legal team representing the men has said it will present evidence from witnesses who include former IRA members, as well as retired police officers and soldiers. Two witnesses have been granted anonymity and will be screened from view.

Adams, 77, will testify in his defence next week. He has stated that he anticipates witnesses will give hearsay evidence against him due to his senior republican role during the conflict, but maintains he had no involvement in the bombings.

The victims' lawyers have said it will be the first time Adams will be cross-examined in an English court about his alleged leadership role in the IRA. The judge will rule on the issue of liability 'on the balance of probabilities.'

The action commenced in 2022, just before the Legacy Act banned new civil claims related to the Troubles, although this is in the process of being reversed. The claimants have raised more than £100,000 through crowdfunding to bring the case, stating they are acting not just for themselves, but for all IRA victims.

Due to a pre-trial ruling, Adams is unable to recover his legal costs from the claimants, believed to be six figures, should he successfully defend the action.
