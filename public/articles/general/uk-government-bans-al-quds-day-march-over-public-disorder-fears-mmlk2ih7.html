UK Government Bans Al Quds Day March Over Public Disorder Fears

The UK government has banned the annual Al Quds Day march in London due to concerns over public disorder, with a stationary demonstration to be held instead under strict conditions.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 11, 2026 · Source: BBC News

The UK government has approved a request from the Metropolitan Police to ban the annual Al Quds Day march in London due to concerns over public disorder.

Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood said the ban is necessary to prevent 'serious public disorder' at the event, which has been seen as pro-Iranian by some, although organisers claim it is a pro-Palestinian event.

Mahmood added that a stationary demonstration could take place instead under strict conditions, and that the police will be able to apply strict conditions if the demonstration proceeds.

The Metropolitan Police said the threshold to ban a protest is high, and that it had not taken its decision lightly.

Faisal Bodi, of the Islamic Human Rights Commission, which organises the event, said a ban would be a 'sad day for freedom of expression'.

Assistant Commissioner Ade Adelekan, Public Order lead for the Met, said the ban applies to the 'Al Quds march and any associated counter-protest marches' and will be in place from 16:00 on Wednesday to last one month.

The decision to ban the march has been met with criticism from some, who argue that it undermines freedom of expression.

The Al Quds march has been a contentious event in the past, with previous marches resulting in arrests for supporting terrorist organisations and antisemitic hate crimes.

The Metropolitan Police said it has safely policed hundreds of protests from across the political spectrum, but that this march raises unique risks and challenges due to the extreme tensions between different factions and the volatile situation in the Middle East.
