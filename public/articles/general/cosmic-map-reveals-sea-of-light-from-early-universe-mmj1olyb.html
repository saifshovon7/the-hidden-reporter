Cosmic Map Reveals 'Sea of Light' from Early Universe

The Hidden Reporter Staff March 7, 2026 Source: Live Science

Astronomers have created one of the most accurate and comprehensive cosmic maps ever made, revealing a brilliant 'sea of light' that permeated the early universe.

The 3D map is composed of light emitted by hydrogen, the simplest and most abundant element in the universe, which emits specific wavelengths of light when excited by nearby stars.

By measuring this light across a vast patch of sky, researchers gained a glimpse of what the universe looked like 9 billion to 11 billion years ago, during an epoch of vigorous star formation.

The new research, part of the Hobby-Eberly Telescope Dark Energy Experiment (HETDEX), aims to illuminate how dark energy and gravity shape the universe.

Using a technique called line-intensity mapping, researchers focused on specific wavelengths emitted by hydrogen atoms, allowing them to chart the concentration and distribution of luminous galaxies and glowing gas clouds.

This approach enables astronomers to gather integrated data from multiple galaxies and intergalactic gas clouds simultaneously, providing a broader understanding of the universe.

Study co-author Julian Muñoz, a theoretical cosmologist, likened the process to viewing a scene through a smudged plane window, capturing all the light and not just the brightest spots.
