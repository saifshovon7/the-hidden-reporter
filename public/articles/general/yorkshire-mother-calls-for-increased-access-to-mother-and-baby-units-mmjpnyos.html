Yorkshire Mother Calls for Increased Access to Mother and Baby Units

A Yorkshire mother is campaigning for improved access to mother and baby units after being forced to travel 90 miles for emergency care during a mental health crisis.

The Hidden Reporter Staff · March 9, 2026 · Source: BBC News

A new mother from Yorkshire is advocating for improved access to specialist units caring for women and babies after she was forced to travel 90 miles for emergency care during a mental health crisis.

Four days after giving birth, 30-year-old Lizzy Berryman's postpartum psychosis forced her to be sectioned and taken from York to Derby while 'soaked in urine' in a hospital car.

Lizzy's experience has sparked a call for better access to mother and baby units (MBU) in the region. According to NHS information, some women from Yorkshire have been sent as far as London for specialist care due to availability issues.

Lizzy, who has since recovered fully and returned to work, praised MBU staff in Derby for 'saving' her life, but is campaigning for more MBUs across the country.

Responding to a BBC Freedom of Information request, NHS data showed around 100 women from across Yorkshire were sent to MBUs outside of their local area over the last five years - with some forced to travel to Birmingham and London.

Aylesbury MP Laura Kyrke-Smith, whose close friend took her own life after having a baby, called access a 'postcode lottery' with some areas 'completely lacking' in specialist care.

Lizzy's experience has highlighted the need for increased access to specialist care for new mothers experiencing mental health issues.

She said, 'It's lit a fire in me to get the word out there that this is a problem and that it is so needed, because there are so many families torn apart.'

Lizzy's family restrained her at home for two hours while waiting for an ambulance, with Lizzy sectioned under the Mental Health Act at York Hospital's emergency department.

She was sent to the closest available unit able to treat psychosis and severe postnatal depression, where she began eight weeks of intensive treatment.

Lizzy's husband Adam and her relatives spent thousands of pounds on nearby accommodation, with Adam's paternity leave ending and his daily visits having to stop.

Lizzy felt lonely and isolated during her treatment, missing out on important milestones with her husband and baby.

However, she gradually stabilised and her section was lifted, but believes being so far from home prolonged her recovery.

'I was so vulnerable at that time and I needed to be somewhere I could feel safe,' she said.

Lizzy's experience has raised awareness about the need for increased access to specialist care for new mothers experiencing mental health issues.
